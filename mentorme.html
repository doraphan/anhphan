CISCO MENTORME PROGRAM

Hosted by Cisco, MentorMe Program gives female students from technology and business area insightful knowledge to the IT world. Each student who signs up as a mentee will be connected to a mentor who is an expert in their area. In other words, MentorMe is the connecting bridge for students and their potential exposure to practical IT/Business setting via tailored guidance, experience and advice from Cisco mentors.

The 12-week program is designed with the focus of assisting the mentees in discovering their career path and equipping them with essential skills for interviews. Additionally, as networking is also crucial to achieving professional goals, MentorMe also allows mentees to build their relationship with their mentors and other fellow mentees. Statistics showed that in 2019, there were 186 mentees and 183 mentors, which are outstanding figures!

  • Four: I worked for four IT companies in my 5 years of professional career. In other words, at average, I spent 1 year and a few months for each of them. I am fully aware that to some people, I could be a typical so-called job hopper. But how can I hide it away when each employer I had worked with, I learned valuable lessons that could benefit my future career prospect, earned recognitions and especially made wonderful network? The first company that I joined is a Japan-invested company. As a fresh graduate, I was lucky to be in a team of experienced senior and there is a project that always sits on the top of my CV. It is an online portal that connects between Vietnam and Japan. Particularly, if a Japanese enterprise wants to seek for a partner in Vietnam, they will look up in the website. According to Google Analytics, it hit more than 10,000 sessions per day and there were more than 1,000 companies that I wrote their information and entered to the website till I left. My second company is a start-up IT comprised of millennials. They are lovely friends that I am happy that we have stayed connected till now. Thanks to them, I knew how to code and differentiate coding languages. There was a legendary joke that I used to tell people so that they had some ideas how clueless I had been before:


    Boss: "Hey Anh, we are lacking human resources. For our new projects, we must have more coders. Can you help me to draft some job recruitments? After you finish, ask other guys to check it for you."

    Me: "Sure sir."

    After composing the job recruitment as per my boss' request, I sent it to my IT colleagues to double check it before posting it on the recruitment platforms. Since it is an IT job recruitment, they would be more familiar with it than me.

    My recruitment post: "Hi, we are Pure Solutions and we need some people who know how to do Ruby on Nails to join our team. Fresh, young and fun environment. You will love to work with us."

    My colleagues: "You have just turned our company into a salon. It is Ruby on Rails, not Ruby on Nails."


    It is surely an unforgettable memory to me. After quitting the company, I decided to have a break and remotely worked for the Japan-based company of my former Japanese boss in which I was responsible for writing Vietnamese content for a Japanese tourism website. As a Japan-holic, this job fascinated me and my social media skills boosted its visitors significantly and made my boss happy. However, the website was shut down because the top managers wanted to shift the business into e-commerce, hence, leaving no job for tourism information. Inevitably, I left not only the job... but also a great impression to my manager who was very supportive of my further education pursuit in Australia. Having accumulated much digital experience, I landed the same job position in an Australia-owned company which was also my last company before my flying to Australia. If the first Japanese company preferred PHP, the second Vietnamese one used Ruby on Rails, then in the last Australia one, ASP.NET was their language. Under the supervision of my Australian boss, my writing skills got enhanced and particularly shaped to the Australian audience. I was delighted that I did not allow my job title to frame me into same repetitive writing tasks. Not only my writing, I also had exposure to practice design and coding. In here, it instilled on me a new passion for data.
